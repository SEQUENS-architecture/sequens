Beach package · for Leo

Beach diary

Writing a recount · ~40 minutes · themed for Leo · logs to his coverage map

First thing

Talk it over

Ask Leo about the best bit of the beach. What happened first? Just chat - you're warming up his memory.

Show him how

Model a recount

Show how we tell things in order - "first… then… after that…". Do one sentence about your day as an example.

Do it together

Tell the day aloud

Say the beach trip together, in order, before any writing. Count it on your fingers - three or four big moments.

Quick check

Right order?

Mix up three moments and see if Leo can put them back in order. If yes, he's ready to write.

His turn

Write the diary

Leo writes his beach diary using the sheet. The "fastest find" challenge keeps his fast-blue-hero energy in it.

Last thing

Read it back

Read it together and celebrate one brilliant sentence. That's the bit he'll remember.

Year 9 · History · for Sam

Why did war break out in 1914?

Causes of the First World War · ~60 min · a full curriculum lesson, themed for Sam

National Curriculum link: KS3 History - "the First World War and the Second World War" (challenges for Britain, Europe and the wider world, 1901-present). Historical concept: cause & consequence. (2014 programmes of study - the current statutory curriculum.)
First thing

The spark

June 1914: one assassination in Sarajevo. Recall who Franz Ferdinand was - and why a single death could matter this much.

Show him how

Model the long-term causes - MAIN

Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism. Build them on the Spokes Frame as the pressures that were already building before 1914.

Do it together

Connect the spark to the system

Use "so that…" chains: the alliance system meant a local crisis pulled in the great powers, so that within weeks six empires were at war.

Quick check · hinge

Catch the misconception

"Which BEST explains why one assassination became a world war?" - A) it was simply a tragic murder · B) the alliance system dragged the great powers in · C) Germany alone wanted war · D) it was inevitable. Many choosing A or C → re-model the alliance web.

His turn

Build an argument

Sam writes a paragraph prioritising the most significant cause and justifying it - colour-coded support, from sentence stems to a counter-argument challenge.

Last thing

Rank & justify

Exit task: rank MAIN + the trigger, and finish - "The most significant cause was… because…". A GCSE-style judgement in miniature.

Sam fills the branches with you

📖 Sam's reading Y9 · interest-themed

Picture 1914 Europe as a strategy board where every power has locked into a web of treaties. Pull one thread - a shot in Sarajevo - and the whole board reacts: Austria moves, Russia counters, Germany commits, and within weeks the alliances drag six empires into a war almost no one chose.

Pitched to Sam's reading level and interest (strategy games). Private use; shared exports use neutral framing.

Our approach to Leo’s education

Leo is educated at home through a blend of planned, structured lessons and interest-led, real-world learning. We use the England National Curriculum as a guide for coverage and progression, while allowing Leo’s curiosity to shape how and when topics are explored. Learning takes place through focused sessions, family trips and projects, daily reading, and everyday life - chosen to suit his age, ability and the way he learns best.

What Leo has been learning

English. Leo writes for different purposes - most recently a beach recount using time-order language - and reads fluently at an early-fluent level. He is building stamina in extended writing.

Mathematics. Number and place value, measurement (length, mass, time), and an introduction to equivalent fractions, much of it applied practically through measuring, cooking and telling the time.

Science. Living things and their habitats (grouping and classification, explored at rock pools), the parts and life cycle of plants, and how we see light - hands-on and enquiry-led.

Foundation subjects. History, geography, art and PSHE covered broadly through projects and trips - local history, coasts and the water cycle, and personal safety.

Learning beyond the curriculum

This year Leo completed 12 cross-curricular real-world projects, including a full day’s study at the beach, a nature classification walk, baking (following a method and measuring accurately), and a local museum visit. These develop curiosity, independence and the ability to apply learning in context - strengths of a home education.

How we record Leo’s learning

We keep an ongoing portfolio: photographs of finished work, written pieces, and short voice recordings of Leo reading aloud and explaining his thinking. A representative sample is available on request.

Focus for the year ahead

  • Extend written pieces with more detail and a wider range of connectives.
  • Secure equivalent fractions and progress to comparing and ordering fractions.
  • Continue building scientific vocabulary through hands-on enquiry.
